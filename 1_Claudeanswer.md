Here is what I think the three sources actually contribute structurally, and why they are genuinely compatible rather than just layered on top of each other:

**Rawls** provides the *legitimation layer* — the answer to "why should someone trust this project?" The veil of ignorance test is especially useful because it works whether the project is AI-assisted or not, whether the topic is financial or geopolitical, whether the producer is an individual or an institution. It gives a portable ethical ground.

**The Star Trek bible** provides the *operational layer* — the answer to "how do you maintain quality across contributors and cycles without the creator being in the room every time?" This is where the persona system, source tiers, and output templates live. It is also where the parallel-contribution insight comes from: quality does not require centralization, it requires *documented constraints*.

**The Claude Constitution** provides the *epistemic hygiene layer* — the answer to "how do you ensure the outputs are honest rather than merely well-organized?" Calibrated uncertainty, non-manipulation, and autonomy preservation are not AI-specific principles; they are good epistemic practice for any knowledge production project. They happen to be clearly articulated in that document.

**Rawls also does something the other two don't**: the difference principle applied to information access gives the democratization commitment an ethical foundation that is not just "it would be nice to share" but "inequality of access to structured analysis is only justified if it produces better outcomes for those with least access." That is a stronger claim, and it shapes the dissemination ethics section meaningfully.
